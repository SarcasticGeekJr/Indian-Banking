Chapter 01
The Structure of Indian Banking
Who the players are, how they're categorised, and why the government owns so many banks.
The quick history you need

Indian banking has three eras. Before 1969: mostly private banks, British-era legacy, urban-focused. 1969–1991: Indira Gandhi's government nationalised 14 major private banks in 1969 and 6 more in 1980, turning them into Public Sector Banks. The goal was to bring banking to rural India. 1991 onwards: economic liberalisation opened the door for new private banks. HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, Axis Bank — all entered the market in the 1990s and transformed Indian banking with technology and service quality.

// Analogy

Think of 1969 nationalisation like the government taking over all the major restaurants in the country and saying "now we'll make sure food reaches every village." It worked for reach, but private restaurants that entered later are faster, cleaner, and more innovative. Both now coexist.

// Jargon
Scheduled Bank — A bank listed in the Second Schedule of the RBI Act, 1934. Meets minimum capital requirements. Can borrow from RBI and is part of the formal clearing system. 124 of 128 commercial banks are scheduled.
Nationalisation — Government taking ownership and control of private companies. In 1969 and 1980, India nationalised its major private banks to make banking accessible to rural populations.
PSB (Public Sector Bank) — A bank where the central government owns more than 50% of the equity. SBI alone has ~25% market share in deposits and advances.
NBFC (Non-Banking Financial Company) — A company that provides financial services like loans but does not hold a banking license. Cannot accept demand deposits or savings accounts from the public. Examples: Bajaj Finance, Muthoot Finance.
Payments Bank — A special RBI-licensed entity that can receive deposits and do payments but cannot lend. Think of it as a very restricted bank. Revolut's closest Indian equivalent before it got its PPI license.
MSME — Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises. Small businesses. A huge segment of India's economy that banks traditionally underserved — which is why NBFCs and SFBs exist.
SBI — why it dominates everything

State Bank of India is not just India's largest bank — it is one of the largest banks in the world. It has over 22,000 branches, 58,000+ ATMs, and holds approximately 25% of all bank deposits and loans in India. It was formed in 1955 from the Imperial Bank of India, which itself came from three Presidency Banks dating back to 1806.

When people say "my salary account is in SBI," that's the primary account relationship Revolut needs to eventually displace. That's the scale of the incumbent problem.

Deposit insurance in India — DICGC

India has deposit insurance through DICGC — Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation, a subsidiary of RBI. If a licensed bank collapses, each depositor gets back up to ₹5 lakh per bank.

This is important for Revolut: their PPI wallet is not DICGC-insured. Your money is safeguarded but not insured. As and when Revolut applies for a full banking license in India, DICGC protection becomes a major trust and marketing lever.

// Jargon
DICGC — Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation. India's deposit insurance scheme. Protects up to ₹5 lakh per depositor per bank. Equivalent to FSCS in the UK.
KYC (Know Your Customer) — Mandatory identity verification all financial institutions must perform before opening accounts. In India, Aadhaar + PAN is the standard KYC combination. Revolut India is fully Aadhaar-KYC compliant.
HNI (High Net Worth Individual) — A customer with significant investable wealth. Foreign banks in India primarily serve HNIs and large corporates rather than retail customers.

RBI in depth — what it actually does

The Reserve Bank of India, established April 1, 1935, is India's central bank. It is owned by the Government of India (Ministry of Finance). It is both the country's economic brain and the banking industry's traffic police.

1. Issues currency — All ₹10 to ₹500 notes are printed and authorised by RBI. Coins are issued by the government separately.

2. Sets monetary policy — Controls interest rates through repo rate, CRR, and SLR to manage inflation and economic growth.

3. Regulates banks — Licenses banks, sets capital requirements, audits them, and cancels licenses if they fail (as it did with Paytm Payments Bank in 2024).

4. Manages foreign exchange — Controls how money flows in and out of India under FEMA. Any company like Revolut moving money across borders must comply.

5. Banker to banks — Commercial banks keep accounts with RBI. When a bank is in crisis, RBI lends to it as "lender of last resort."

6. Banker to government — Manages India's debt, government accounts, and foreign exchange reserves.

// Key RBI rate jargon
Repo Rate — The rate at which RBI lends money to commercial banks overnight. When RBI raises this, banks borrow more expensively → they raise their own lending rates → home loans and personal loans get costlier → people borrow less → inflation cools. Currently 6.25%.
Reverse Repo Rate — The rate RBI pays banks when they park excess money with RBI. When banks prefer parking money with RBI over lending, money flow in the economy slows.
CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio) — A % of a bank's deposits that it must keep with RBI as cash. Banks cannot lend this money. Currently ~4%. RBI raises CRR to pull money out of the system and reduce inflation.
SLR (Statutory Liquidity Ratio) — A % of deposits that banks must keep in liquid assets (government bonds, gold). Not with RBI, but cannot be lent out either. Currently ~18%.
MPC (Monetary Policy Committee) — The 6-member committee (3 RBI + 3 external) that decides the repo rate every 2 months. Created in 2016. Sets the cost of money in India.
Inflation (CPI) — Consumer Price Index. Measures how much everyday goods cost vs. last year. RBI's primary mandate is keeping CPI inflation around 4% (+/- 2%). When inflation rises, RBI raises rates.
NPCI — the unsung hero of Indian payments

NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) is not a bank and not a regulator. It's a non-profit infrastructure company set up jointly by RBI and the Indian Banks' Association. It owns and operates the payment rails that make India's digital payments work.

NPCI built and runs: UPI (real-time payments), RuPay (India's homegrown card network), NEFT and RTGS (bank transfer systems), IMPS (instant bank-to-bank transfers), FASTag (highway toll payments), and NACH (automated recurring payment mandates).

// Analogy

NPCI is like NHAI (National Highways Authority of India) — it built the roads. All the cars (PhonePe, Google Pay, Paytm, banks, Revolut) just drive on NPCI's roads. The roads are free and open to everyone, which is why India's payments are so competitive and cheap.

// Jargon
NPCI — National Payments Corporation of India. Non-profit that owns India's payment infrastructure. Set up by RBI + IBA in 2008.
RuPay — India's own card payment network, like Visa or Mastercard but Indian. Created by NPCI. Lower cost for merchants. Used in ~60% of debit cards in India. Mandated for Jan Dhan accounts.
FEMA — Foreign Exchange Management Act. The law governing all cross-border money movement in India. Revolut's AD II license is a FEMA license.
AD II License — Authorised Dealer Category II License. Allows a company to offer foreign exchange services (forex cards, remittances) in India. Revolut obtained this in 2022.
IBA — Indian Banks' Association. The representative body of all Indian banks. Co-founder of NPCI with RBI.

Before UPI, there were three systems for moving money between bank accounts. They still coexist today, each serving a different purpose.

// Jargon
NEFT — Works in half-hourly batches. No minimum or maximum amount. Free for online transfers. Used when speed is not critical — e.g. paying a vendor.
RTGS — Every transaction settled individually and instantly in RBI's books. Minimum ₹2 lakh. Used for high-value transactions — real estate, corporate payments.
IMPS — Instant bank-to-bank transfer. Predates UPI. Still widely used for salary/vendor payments up to ₹5 lakh via banking apps.
NACH — National Automated Clearing House. Used for recurring automatic debits — EMI payments, insurance premiums, utility bills. You sign a NACH mandate and it auto-debits every month. India's version of a standing order.
IFSC Code — Indian Financial System Code. 11-character identifier for each bank branch. Required for NEFT, RTGS, IMPS transfers. Format: HDFC0001234 — first 4 = bank name, 5th = 0, last 6 = branch code. UPI made this irrelevant for daily use.
Foreign exchange in India — the LRS framework

In India, you cannot freely send unlimited money abroad. RBI regulates cross-border money movement under FEMA. The key scheme for retail customers is LRS (Liberalised Remittance Scheme) — Indian residents can send up to $250,000 per financial year abroad for permitted purposes: education, travel, investments, gifts.

Banks and licensed money changers (AD II license holders like Revolut) facilitate these remittances. When a student going abroad loads a forex card with USD or GBP, they're using LRS. Revolut's India forex card operates entirely within this framework.

// TCS on LRS — a PMM opportunity

Since October 2023, the government charges 20% TCS (Tax Collected at Source) on LRS remittances above ₹7 lakh per year for most purposes (travel, investments). This made international spending significantly more expensive and controversial. While TCS is refundable via tax filing, users feel the immediate cash flow impact. Revolut India's messaging around "transparent, lower-cost international spending" is directly relevant here.

// Jargon
LRS (Liberalised Remittance Scheme) — RBI scheme allowing Indian residents to send up to $250,000 abroad per year for permitted purposes. Revolut's international FX card operates under LRS.
TCS (Tax Collected at Source) — A tax the seller collects from the buyer at the time of sale, and deposits with the government. On LRS remittances above ₹7 lakh, the rate is 20% for most purposes. Refundable via ITR but reduces immediate liquidity.
Forex Card — A prepaid card loaded with foreign currency (USD, GBP, EUR) that you use abroad. Sold by banks and AD II holders. Revolut's international card is this product — differentiated by its interbank FX rate with no weekend markup.
Remittances — Money sent between countries. India is the world's largest remittance recipient (Indians abroad sent $129B home in 2024). Outward remittances (Indians sending money abroad — students, investors, travelers) is also massive and growing.
AD II (Authorised Dealer Category II) — RBI license allowing non-banks to offer forex cards and cross-border remittances. Revolut India has held this since 2022 — it's what enabled the international FX card before the PPI license.

UPI & India Stack — India's Payment Revolution
UPI is the most important thing to understand about Indian fintech. Nothing else comes close.
What UPI is and why it's extraordinary

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) was launched in April 2016 by NPCI. It's a real-time payment system that links your bank account to your mobile number — letting you send or receive money instantly, 24/7/365, for free.

Before UPI, to send money to a friend you needed their account number, IFSC code, had to add them as a beneficiary (wait 30 minutes for verification), then transfer. The process took at least an hour. After UPI: type their phone number. Done. Money lands in 2 seconds.

// Why UPI is different from Venmo or GPay in the US

The US has Venmo. The UK has Faster Payments. But those are private, closed systems. UPI is a public highway that every app can drive on — PhonePe, Google Pay, Paytm, your bank's own app, and now Revolut — all using the same infrastructure, interoperably. No walled gardens. That's what makes it revolutionary and why India accounts for nearly half of all global real-time payments.

How UPI works — step by step
// What happens when you pay ₹200 to a chai stall via PhonePe
You open PhonePe and scan the stall's QR code
PhonePe is a TPAP (Third Party App Provider) on the UPI network
Enter ₹200 and your UPI PIN (4 or 6 digits)
UPI PIN is set during setup — it authenticates every transaction
PhonePe sends payment request to NPCI via UPI APIs
NPCI routes to your bank (e.g. SBI) to debit your account
SBI verifies PIN, debits ₹200 from your account
Sends confirmation back through NPCI
NPCI credits ₹200 to the stall's bank account
Voice box announces "Do sau rupees received." Entire process: under 2 seconds.
// UPI Jargon
UPI ID / VPA (Virtual Payment Address) — Your unique payment address on UPI. Like an email address for money. Example: 9876543210@paytm or sakthi@okicici. You share this instead of your bank account number.
TPAP (Third Party App Provider) — Apps that provide a UPI interface without being banks themselves. PhonePe, Google Pay, Paytm are TPAPs. They use NPCI's rails but don't hold the money — your bank does.
P2P (Person to Person) — Sending money directly to another person. Friend, family, etc.
P2M (Person to Merchant) — Paying a shop, restaurant, or business. P2M now exceeds P2P on UPI — 62% of transactions are P2M as of 2024. This is where the market battle is.
QR Code — The square barcode merchants display. Customers scan to pay. India has over 500 million UPI-enabled QR codes deployed — more than most countries' total card terminals.
UPI Lite — Offline UPI for small transactions (up to ₹500) that works without internet. Uses a local wallet on your phone. Launched to solve low-connectivity areas.
The UPI market battle Revolut is entering

Three players dominate UPI: PhonePe (~47% market share), Google Pay (~37%), and Paytm (~8%). Together ~92%. NPCI imposed a 30% market cap rule to prevent monopoly — but has repeatedly extended the compliance deadline. Effectively, these three have locked up the daily payments habit.

This is the wall Revolut faces. Not a technology wall — UPI is open to everyone. A habit wall. Indians already have a UPI app they use dozens of times a day. The bar for switching is very high. Revolut cannot compete head-on in daily UPI — it needs to own the global dimension that PhonePe, GPay, and Paytm cannot offer.

India Stack — the bigger digital infrastructure

India Stack is the government's broader digital infrastructure programme. UPI is just one layer. There are four:

Presence-less layer — Aadhaar: India's biometric identity system. 1.4 billion Indians enrolled. Allows instant, paperless identity verification using fingerprint or iris scan. Aadhaar eKYC is how Revolut India verifies customers without physical documents.

Paperless layer — DigiLocker: Digital document storage linked to Aadhaar. PAN card, driving license, degree certificates all stored digitally. Reduces document submission friction.

Cashless layer — UPI: The real-time payments backbone described above.

Consent layer — Account Aggregator (AA): A newer framework allowing customers to securely share their financial data (bank statements, loan history) with other financial institutions — with explicit consent. Enables faster loan approvals and personalised products. India's version of Open Banking.

// India Stack jargon
Aadhaar — India's 12-digit biometric identity number. Linked to fingerprint and iris scan. 1.4 billion Indians enrolled. Used for bank KYC, SIM activation, government subsidies, tax filing. The foundational identity layer of India Stack.
eKYC (Electronic KYC) — Instant identity verification using Aadhaar OTP or biometric. Replaced physical document submission. Allows fintechs to onboard customers in minutes. Critical for Revolut India's onboarding flow.
PAN (Permanent Account Number) — 10-character alphanumeric tax identity number issued by Income Tax Department. Required for opening bank accounts, investments, large transactions. Aadhaar + PAN = India's standard KYC.
Account Aggregator (AA) — RBI-regulated framework where customers consent to share financial data across institutions. Enables open banking. Credit scoring without traditional bank relationships.
JAM Trinity — Jan Dhan (bank accounts) + Aadhaar (identity) + Mobile (connectivity). The three pillars used to bring 500 million unbanked Indians into the formal financial system between 2014–2020.

PPI License — what Revolut actually has, in detail

A PPI (Prepaid Payment Instrument) is exactly what it sounds like — a payment instrument you load money into before spending. Your Ola Money wallet, your Paytm wallet, a gift card — all PPIs. Revolut India can now issue these with full RBI authorisation received April 2025.

With its full PPI license, Revolut India can issue prepaid cards (physical and virtual), operate a PPI wallet where customers load and hold money, enable UPI payments from the PPI wallet, and offer the multi-currency forex card alongside the domestic product.

What Revolut cannot do with just a PPI license: offer savings accounts with interest, lend money (no personal loans, credit cards, EMIs), accept deposits like a bank, or offer DICGC insurance on wallet balances.

// The wallet balance cap — a real structural constraint

RBI restricts PPI wallet balance to ₹2 lakh maximum. This is too low for salary deposits. A person's monthly salary in a metro city could easily be ₹1–5 lakh — but keeping it in a PPI wallet doesn't make sense because the ₹2L cap creates friction. A full banking license would remove this. This is the single biggest reason Revolut's India roadmap must include a banking license eventually.

// PPI Jargon
PPI (Prepaid Payment Instrument) — A wallet or card you load money onto before spending. Not a bank account. RBI-licensed. Maximum balance: ₹2 lakh (full KYC). Examples: Paytm wallet, Amazon Pay, PhonePe wallet, now Revolut India wallet.
Full KYC PPI — A PPI where the customer has completed full Aadhaar + PAN identity verification. Allows higher limits (₹2 lakh), UPI access, and cash withdrawals. Revolut India requires full KYC.
Minimum KYC PPI — A wallet with just a mobile number. Limited to ₹10,000 balance, no cash withdrawals, no P2P transfers. Very restricted — mostly used for quick onboarding in low-bandwidth situations.
PPI-UPI Interoperability — RBI mandated in 2022 that PPI wallets must be interoperable with UPI. This means you can pay from your Revolut wallet to any UPI QR code or bank account. Critical for Revolut's daily use case.

The three positioning angles for Revolut India

Revolut cannot win India by being another UPI payments app. The PMM challenge is finding the angle where Revolut's global capabilities create unique, defensible value.

1. The globally-connected Indian. Students studying abroad, professionals working with global companies, frequent travelers, NRI family members managing money across borders. These people need both domestic UPI and international FX in one app. Nobody offers this seamlessly. This is Revolut's clearest right-to-win.

2. The premium-aspiring urban professional. Uses Zerodha for stocks, Groww for SIPs, GPay for daily payments — currently 5 apps for one financial life. Revolut's super-app positioning could resonate deeply if the product execution matches the promise.

3. The outward remittance sender. Indians sending money abroad for education, travel, investments. LRS framework is the gateway. TCS on LRS is a genuine, visible pain point. If Revolut can help users understand and optimise this, it's a real PMM message.
